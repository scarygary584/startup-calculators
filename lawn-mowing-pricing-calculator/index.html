Lawn care profit example

Say a mowing visit is priced at $60, takes 45 minutes on site, and has $10 in travel and fuel. If you value your labor at $35 per hour and add a repair reserve for equipment wear, the real profit is lower than the price tag suggests. That is why route density matters: two nearby lawns can beat one higher-priced lawn across town.

How to use the numbers

Start with the default $60 visit, then adjust job time, route/fuel cost, and owner pay until the result matches your local market. A small lawn beside another customer can be profitable at a lower price than the same lawn across town.

Truth-check

Drive time, scheduling gaps, rain, repairs, sharpening blades, small lots, tall grass, and customer churn can reduce profit. Equipment replacement and vehicle wear are real owner costs, even when customers only see one flat mowing price.

Certification / license note

Basic mowing is often low-barrier, but business registration, insurance, pesticide rules, water rules, and local regulations may matter.

FAQ

How do I calculate lawn care profit?

Start with the mowing price, then subtract travel, fuel, labor value, equipment wear, repairs, supplies, and other direct costs. Then compare the leftover profit with the time required.

How much should I charge to mow a lawn?

A mowing price should cover the time on the yard, trimming, cleanup, travel, fuel, equipment wear, labor value, overhead, and profit. The calculator helps you test that number before you quote the job.

What affects lawn care profit?

Yard size, grass height, slope, edging, gates, pets, drive time, fuel, equipment repairs, and route density can all change the profit. Two lawns that look similar can have very different math.

Should I charge by the hour or by the yard?

Many operators quote a flat yard price, but they still use hourly math in the background. Time-based math helps protect you from taking jobs that look fine but pay poorly after travel and expenses.

Can pesticide or weed control work require licensing?

Yes. Pesticide and chemical application rules vary by location. Do not assume mowing rules and chemical application rules are the same.
